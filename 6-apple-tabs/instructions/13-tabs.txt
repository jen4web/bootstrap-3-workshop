From the starting apples page, add tabs to the page as follows:

1. Add class of "nav nav-tabs" to the UL with ID of tabNav

2. Add data-toggle="tab" to each A in the UL

3. Link each href in the A in the UL to each ID (overview, botanical, wild, genome)

4. Surround the overview, botanical, wild, genome div's with IDs with a single div with class of "tab-content"

5. For the overview div, add classes of tab-pane and active. For wild, genome, and botanical divs, add a class of tab-pane

6. View in browser -- links work, but the active tab is not highlighted until some clicking around happens.

7. To make the active tab truly active, must be initialized with jQuery. After the bootstrap.min.js call at the bottom of the page, add the following:

    <script>
        $(function () {
         $('#tabNav a:first').tab('show');
         });
    </script>


'#tabNav a:first' is a selector that selects the first tab. If you wish to select a different tab, substitute an appropriate selector here. (Could also say '#tabNav #overview')

            <h3>Overview</h3>
            <p>The <strong>apple</strong> is the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pome" title="Pome">pomaceous</a> <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> of the apple <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a>, species <em><strong>Malus domestica</strong></em> in the rose <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> (<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosaceae" title="Rosaceae">Rosaceae</a>). It is one of the most widely <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tillage" title="Tillage">cultivated</a> tree fruits, and the most widely known of the many members of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <em><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malus" title="Malus">Malus</a></em> that are used by humans. Apples grow on small, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> trees. The tree originated in<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, where its wild ancestor, <em><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malus_sieversii" title="Malus sieversii">Malus sieversii</a></em>, is still found today. Apples have been grown for thousands of years in Asia and Europe, and were brought to North America by European colonists. Apples have been present in the mythology and religions of many cultures, including <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse</a>, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek</a> and Christian traditions. In 2010, the fruit's <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> was decoded, leading to new understandings of disease control and selective breeding in apple production.</p>

            <h3>Botanical Information</h3>
            <p>The apple forms a tree that is small and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a>, reaching 3 to 12 metres (9.8 to 39 ft) tall, with a broad, often densely twiggy crown.<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple#cite_note-app-3">[3]</a> The <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> are <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leaf_arrangement" title="Leaf arrangement">alternately arranged</a> simple ovals 5 to 12 cm long and 3–6 centimetres (1.2–2.4 in) broad on a 2 to 5 centimetres (0.79 to 2.0 in) <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petiole_%28botany%29" title="Petiole (botany)">petiole</a> with an acute tip, serrated margin and a slightly downy underside. <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blossom" title="Blossom">Blossoms</a> are produced in spring simultaneously with the budding of the leaves.   The flowers are white with a pink tinge that gradually fades, five <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petal" title="Petal">petaled</a>,   and 2.5 to 3.5 centimetres (0.98 to 1.4 in) in diameter. The fruit   matures in autumn, and is typically 5 to 9 centimetres (2.0 to 3.5 in)   in diameter. The skins of ripe apples range from red to yellow to green   in colouration, and covered in a protective layer of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epicuticular_wax" title="Epicuticular wax">epicuticular wax</a>,<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple#cite_note-4">[4]</a> while the flesh is pale yellowish-white. The center of the fruit contains five <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carpel" title="Carpel">carpels</a> arranged in a <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five-pointed_star" title="Five-pointed star">five-point star</a>, each carpel containing one to three <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a>, called <a href="http://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pip" title="wikt:pip">pips</a>.<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple#cite_note-app-3">[3]</a></p>

            <h3>Wild Ancestors</h3>
            <p>The original wild <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancestor" title="Ancestor">ancestor</a> of <em>Malus domestica</em> was <em>Malus sieversii</em>, found growing wild in the mountains of Central Asia in southern Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang" title="Xinjiang">Xinjiang</a>, China.<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple#cite_note-app-3">[3]</a><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple#cite_note-Architecture_and_size_relations:_an_essay_on_the_apple_.28Malus_x_domestica.2C_Rosaceae.29_tree-5">[5]</a> Cultivation of the species, most likely beginning on the forested flanks of the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tian_Shan" title="Tian Shan">Tian Shan</a> mountains, progressed over a long period of time and permitted secondary <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introgression" title="Introgression">introgression</a> of genes from other species into the open-pollinated seeds. Significant exchange with <em><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malus_sylvestris" title="Malus sylvestris">Malus sylvestris</a></em>,   the crabapple, resulted in current populations of apples to be more   related to crabapples than to the more morphologically similar   progenitor <em>Malus sieversii</em>. In strains without recent admixture the contribution of the latter predominates.<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple#cite_note-6">[6]</a><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple#cite_note-7">[7]</a><a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple#cite_note-Coart-8">[8]</a></p>
        </div>

            <h3>Genome</h3>
            <p>In 2010, an Italian-led consortium announced they had decoded the complete <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> of the apple in collaboration with horticultural genomicists at <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington_State_University" title="Washington State University">Washington State University</a>,<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple#cite_note-9">[9]</a> using the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_delicious" title="Golden delicious">Golden delicious</a> variety.<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple#cite_note-10">[10]</a> It had about 57,000 genes, the highest number of any plant genome studied to date<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple#cite_note-11">[11]</a> and more genes than the human genome (about 30,000).<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple#cite_note-12">[12]</a> This new understanding of the apple genome will help scientists in   identifying genes and gene variants that contribute to resistance to   disease and drought, and other desirable characteristics. Understanding   the genes behind these characteristics will allow scientists to perform   more knowledgeable selective breeding. Decoding the genome also provided   proof that <em>Malus sieversii</em> was the wild ancestor of the domestic apple—an issue that had been long-debated in the scientific community.<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple#cite_note-13">[13]</a></p>
